# PS Proposal

You are translating the scoping document into a narrative that closes deals. The proposal is your sales tool—it sells customers on confidence, timeline, and value.

## When to use

**DO** use PS Proposal when:
- The customer is ready to purchase PS hours
- You need a formal document for their procurement team
- You're competing for a deal against another SI

**DON'T** use when:
- The customer is in discovery (too early)
- They've already signed a generic SOW

## The Proposal Structure

| Section | Purpose | Length |
|---------|---------|--------|
| Executive Summary | Win them in 1 page: what, why, how | 1 page |
| Approach | Your methodology, proven success | 2–3 pages |
| Timeline | Gantt + key milestones | 1 page + diagram |
| Deliverables | Itemized, specific | 1–2 pages |
| Pricing & Terms | Cost, SKU mapping, acceptance criteria | 1 page |
| Team | Your architects & engineers | 0.5 page |

## Executive Summary

**The formula:**
```
[Customer name] is deploying [Zscaler SKU] to [solve X problem].

Zscaler Professional Services will architect and implement:
• [Deliverable 1]
• [Deliverable 2]
• [Deliverable 3]

Success criteria: [Metric 1, Metric 2, Metric 3]

Investment: $[amount] over [timeline]
```

**Make it specific.** "We will deploy Zscaler Internet Access with SSL inspection,
DLP, and firewall rules across 5 data centers, supporting 10,000 users" beats
"Zscaler implementation."

## Approach Section

Explain your **methodology**, not just what you'll do:

```
Zscaler PS uses a 5-gate approach:

Gate 1 (Prep): Stakeholder alignment, architecture review
Gate 2 (Design): Policy framework, integration planning
Gate 3 (Build): System deployment, user provisioning
Gate 4 (Test): UAT, cutover planning
Gate 5 (Handoff): Operations training, support transition

This reduces risk by 75% vs. ad-hoc implementations.
```

Reference past wins: "We've deployed similar architectures for [3 similar companies] in [timeframe]."

## Timeline

**Gantt narrative:**
```
Weeks 1–2: Prep (charter, risk log, architecture kickoff)
Weeks 3–4: Design (policy build, integration testing)
Weeks 5–8: Build (deployment, user provisioning)
Weeks 9–10: Test (UAT, cutover validation)
Weeks 11–12: Handoff (training, runbooks, support transition)

Go-live target: [date] (contingent on customer blocking issue resolution)
```

## Deliverables (Itemized)

```
Included:
✓ 1× architecture workshop (4 hours)
✓ 1× policy framework document (policy templates, 50+ rules)
✓ 2× test environments (staging, UAT)
✓ Admin training (2 days on-site or virtual)
✓ 1× operational runbook (daily/weekly/monthly tasks)
✓ 3× post-go-live support days

Not Included:
✗ Integration with 3rd-party SIEM (recommend separate project)
✗ Custom mobile app testing (scope to 50 internal apps)
✗ Multi-geography deployment (recommend phased approach)
```

## Gotchas

1. **Vague deliverables:** "Admin training" can mean 2 hours or 2 days. Be specific. List count (# of policies, # of users, # of integrations).
2. **Hidden scope in "similar":** If you say "similar to Company X," don't let them assume it's identical. Reference your scoping document.
3. **Timeline wishful thinking:** Customers always ask "Can you do it in 4 weeks?" Be firm: "5 is the minimum for quality. We can accelerate certain phases with additional resources (cost TBD)."
4. **No acceptance criteria:** Every deliverable needs a success metric. "Trained admins" = pass a quiz. "Policy framework" = 80% of business rules codified.
5. **Price on the fly:** Never negotiate price in the proposal conversation. Say: "I'll revise based on your feedback and send an updated proposal."

---

**Pro tip:** The proposal is a sales document, not a legal one. Make it narrative, not bureaucratic. Customers buy from people they trust, not from 50-page RFP responses.
